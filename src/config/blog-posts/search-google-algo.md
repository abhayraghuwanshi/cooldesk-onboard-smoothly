# Why Universal Search is the Future of Browsing

We've been trained to "Google it." Need a recipe? Google it. Need a fact? Google it.

But what happens when you need something *you* know?

*   "Where is that Google Doc I started yesterday?"
*   "Which tab has the research on competitor pricing?"
*   "What was that article I read last week about AI agents?"

Google can't help you there. And neither can your browser's default history bar.

## The Fragmented Web

Our digital lives are scattered. A bit of work in Slack, some in Notion, a lot in Google Docs, and endless open tabs. Finding information *within* your own workflow is harder than finding information on the public web.

This is the "Personal Search Problem."

## Enter Universal Search

The concept of Universal Search isn't new—operating systems like macOS (Spotlight) and Windows (Start) have done it for files. But the browser has lagged behind.

**Universal Browser Search** brings this power to the web. It unifies:

1.  **Open Tabs**: Jump to any tab instantly, no matter which window it's in.
2.  **Browsing History**: Search by content, not just URL or title.
3.  **Bookmarks & Favorites**: Your curated library, instantly accessible.
4.  **Web Apps**: Deep integration to search *inside* tools like Notion or Figma (coming soon to CoolDesk).

## Why It Changes Everything

Once you get used to Universal Search, the old way feels broken.

*   **Speed**: No more clicking through windows or scrolling through tiny tabs.
*   **Flow**: You stay in the keyboard flow. `Cmd+K`, type, enter. Done.
*   **Memory**: You don't need to remember *where* something is, just *what* it is.
